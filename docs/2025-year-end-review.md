## Executive Summary

2025 was the best year in the company's history. Revenue came in at **$4.73M** — 3% above the $4.6M board commitment and 26% above 2024's $3.77M. EBITDA reached breakeven for the full year, fulfilling the primary board covenant from the December 2024 meeting. Books overperformed significantly. Electronics surprised in Q4. Sports missed H2 targets due to a buying error. Return rate improved but did not reach the 7.5% target.

The no-discount policy held for the full year. No site-wide promotional events. The short-term revenue cost was real (Clothing missed by ~$10K) but the long-term signal is encouraging: repeat purchase rate rose to 2.9x for Clothing customers vs 1.2x in 2022.

## Category Results

### Electronics — Above Plan
Full year: $1.52M vs $1.44M target (+5.6%).

The margin improvement story is working. Audio and peripherals grew to 38% of Electronics revenue (up from 29% in 2024). Dynamic pricing, expanded to the full catalog in Q3, contributed an estimated 4.1% margin improvement — above the 3% threshold set for the rollout.

Q4 was exceptional: Black Friday pages were the highest-converting in company history. Average order value in Electronics reached $187 in November — driven by premium headphones and laptops, not commodity accessories.

Return rate: **4.9%** vs 4.8% target. Marginally missed, but Electronics return rate improvement has been consistent for four years. No structural concern.

### Clothing — Marginally Below Plan
Full year: $910K vs $920K target (-1.1%).

The miss is entirely attributable to the January discount removal. A $40K promotional event in January 2024 drove top-line that we chose not to replicate in 2025. Adjusting for this, underlying Clothing demand grew ~5% — ahead of the category's long-term trend.

The loyalty story is the headline. Customers who made a second Clothing purchase had a 2.9x annual repurchase rate and 3.1% return rate vs 7.1% for first-time buyers. The email reorder program (launched Q2 2025) has a 22% conversion rate — our best-performing marketing channel by far.

Return rate: **7.1%** vs 6.8% target. Progress — 0.2pp better than 2024 but 0.3pp short of target. Root cause: new SKUs added in Q3 had a 9.4% return rate in their first 60 days. Normal break-in period. No action required.

### Home — Above Plan
Full year: $672K vs $660K target (+1.8%).

The medium-sized furniture re-listing (Q2 2025, with video content) worked. 14 re-listed SKUs had a 7.6% return rate, below the 8.2% category average. The video content investment ($18K total) was the right call.

"Home refresh" bundles reached 18% of Home revenue — customers buying the 4-item sets have a 6.2% return rate vs 8.1% for individual purchases. Bundle strategy is core to the Home category.

Return rate: **7.9%** vs 7.5% target. Still slightly above target — the remaining problem is medium furniture items shipped by third-party carriers. Working with carriers on packaging improvements.

### Books — Significantly Above Plan
Full year: $905K vs $840K target (+7.7%).

Books was the standout performance of 2025. Two things drove the outperformance:

1. **B2B channel:** Added 8 net new corporate clients (target was 10 net new, starting from 14). Ended 2025 with 22 corporate clients. Combined, B2B revenue reached $312K — the largest contributor to the Books overperformance.

2. **Subscription box:** 1,180 members by December 2025, up from 580 in December 2024. Target was 1,200 members. The gifting behavior around the box drove a strong December (+14% vs plan).

Return rate: **2.1%** vs 2.0% target. Essentially at target. The B2B channel has a 0.4% return rate — once corporate clients commit to a title list, returns are negligible.

### Sports — Marginally Below Plan
Full year: $672K vs $680K target (-1.2%).

H1 was on plan. H2 was the problem: we over-bought summer inventory (outdoor equipment, yoga mats) expecting Sports to accelerate into Q3 as planned. The Q3 back-to-school youth sports sub-category underperformed estimates by 28%. By September, we had ~$90K of excess summer inventory that required 22–30% markdowns to move by year-end.

The markdowns cleared the inventory but hit H2 margin significantly.

Return rate: **8.8%** vs 8.5% target. First year below 9% in Sports history. The catalog refresh (completed Q1 2025) reduced impulse-purchase SKUs and added size guides to all apparel. Progress is real — the structural work is paying off.

## Key Decisions and Lessons Learned

### What worked
- **Discount ban:** Short-term revenue cost was real but manageable. Long-term customer quality is improving.
- **Books B2B:** The $60K marketing budget allocation to corporate outreach returned $312K in B2B revenue. 5:1 return.
- **Dynamic pricing expansion:** 4.1% margin improvement in Electronics. Rolling to Clothing accessories in 2026.
- **Loyalty email program (Clothing):** 22% conversion rate, 2.9x repurchase. Best channel in the business.

### What didn't work
- **Sports H2 buying:** $90K of excess inventory required heavy markdown. The buying budget cap needs to be structural, not discretionary. Fixed in the 2026 plan (hard H2 limit: $110K).
- **Q3 youth sports bet:** Underestimated back-to-school timing and youth-sport parent buying behavior. Moved planning calendar earlier for 2026.

### Board decisions made at December 2025 meeting
1. Approved EMEA UK pilot for Q3 2026 — UK fulfillment partner selection due February 2026.
2. Extended no-site-wide-discount policy for 2026 (third year).
3. Set EBITDA target at positive (not just breakeven) for 2026 — estimated 4% EBITDA margin.
4. Sports H2 buying hard cap: $110K (not discretionary).
5. Books subscription box milestone: 2,500 members by December 2026 is an operating KPI (not a board covenant).

## Going into 2026

The company enters 2026 in its strongest position yet. The unit economics are proven. The Books B2B channel is a structural advantage — 22 corporate clients provide predictable, high-margin recurring revenue. Sports is finally mature enough to be profitable rather than just growing.

The EMEA pilot is the biggest bet in company history. The board was explicit: it is an operational learning exercise, not a revenue commitment. If it works, 2027 could include France and Germany. If it doesn't, the domestic business is strong enough that a failed pilot does not threaten the company.

Q1 2026 will look slow on paper — it always does. The absence of a January promotion means January revenue will be 18–22% below December. This is expected, planned, and the right call. The Q1 priority is building the infrastructure for a strong H2: EMEA vendor contracts, Sports Q1 buying, Books subscription growth, and the July launch readiness.
